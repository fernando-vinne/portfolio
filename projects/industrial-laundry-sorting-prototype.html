Industrial Laundry Sorting Prototype

Mechanical development and deployment support for robotic prototype systems intended to automate part of the soiled-side sorting process in industrial laundries. The project involved aluminum-profile structures, enclosure elements, gripper integration, pneumatic integration, transport-related design, installation support, and validation in a real industrial environment.

Overview

The project focused on building and deploying robotic prototype systems for industrial laundry sorting. In the target process, textiles on the soiled side need to be picked from a moving conveyor and placed into the correct bin. This creates a challenging combination of deformable objects, moving targets, constrained installation space, and real industrial conditions.

Unlike a single component project, this work required system-level thinking: mechanical structure, surrounding parts, gripper integration, pneumatics, transport, installation, accessibility, and validation all had to work together in one prototype that could be installed and tested on site.

My contribution

My work focused on the mechanical design and integration of the prototype system. This included the aluminum-profile frame, surrounding mechanical structures, doors and enclosure elements, gripper integration, transport-related components, pneumatic integration, installation support, and validation after deployment.

  • Designed aluminum-profile structures and surrounding mechanical components.
  • Designed and adapted enclosure elements, doors, and access-related parts.
  • Integrated the gripper and supporting components into the prototype system.
  • Worked on transportation and installation-related mechanical details.
  • Supported pneumatic integration and mechanical validation.
  • Contributed to prototype installation, testing, and gathering learnings for later iterations.

Engineering challenges

Building for deployment

  • The prototype needed to work outside the workshop in a real industrial laundry environment.
  • The mechanical structure had to be robust enough for transport, installation, and on-site testing.
  • Access, safety, enclosure elements, and serviceability had to be considered during mechanical design.
  • The system had to integrate robot, gripper, conveyor interaction, pneumatics, and surrounding structures.

Learning through real use

  • The first prototype was used to understand what changes when a concept leaves the workshop.
  • Real deployment introduced additional constraints that were not fully visible during early development.
  • The second prototype incorporated learnings from the first installation and accounted for further requirements.
  • The process helped translate prototype testing into practical product-development feedback.

Design process

1
Define the prototype task

The system needed to pick textiles from a moving conveyor and sort them into the correct bin, while fitting into the constraints of an industrial laundry environment.

2
Design the mechanical structure

Developed aluminum-profile structures and surrounding mechanical parts to support the robot, gripper, enclosure elements, access requirements, and integration into the overall setup.

3
Integrate subsystems

Worked on the mechanical integration of the gripper, doors, transport-related elements, pneumatic components, and supporting structures.

4
Install and validate on site

Supported installation and validation of the prototype in a real industrial environment, where practical constraints and new requirements became visible.

5
Iterate toward the next prototype

The prototype ran for a period of time, learnings were gathered, and the results informed the development of the next iteration with improved requirements and mechanical decisions.

Prototype development

Result

Two prototype iterations were installed and tested in a real industrial environment. Each prototype ran for a period of time, was used to gather practical deployment learnings, and then informed the development of the next iteration.

This project was especially valuable because it covered the full path from concept and mechanical design to installation, validation, and iteration based on real-world prototype feedback.
